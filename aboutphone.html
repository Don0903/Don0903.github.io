What happens to my phone?

Used smartphones have potential to be economically profitable and environmentally friendly. Here's a snippet on how raw materials can be extracted!

Indium From LCD's!

Indium is one of the rarest raw material on earth with a very high demand, which makes it very important to recycle

Step 1


The LCDs screens were taken apart to perform the Indium extraction. Indium is a precious metal with relatively limited natural reserves which makes it important to recycle. The LCDs were removed from the screen with a hot gun to remove the glue. After the separation, the LCDs were placed in an acetone bath for a few hours to remove the polymeric polarising film.


The LCDs were then broken in small parts with a mortar a pestle to transform them in a powder.



Step 2


The dried powder was leached by using a solution of 2L of H2SO4 with a concentration of 0,5 M for 2h in four 1L Erlenmeyer. The powder weighted 34 grams.


After the two hours, each Erlenmeyer went through a precipitation with NH4OH to obtain a PH of 7,4. The Erlenmeyer were left for two days to precipitate, a few very small residues of indium could be seen at the bottom of each Erlenmeyer.


The Indium was collected by filtration and the filter paper was weighted to deduce the final mass of Indium collected in 34g of LCDs. The final weight of Indium is around 0,05g, due to impurities, it was difficult to deduce a yield.

How does your phone impact the environment?

Through an OpenLCA modelling tool, the environmental impact was conducted for the main metal components of the mobile phone by analysing the recycling end of life treatment as well as the production/mining of the materials and comparing them.
